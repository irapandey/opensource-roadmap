Open Source, One Step at a Time: Choosing the Right Project
Open source is not short of projects.
The real challenge is figuring out where to start.
A good first project removes friction and helps you build momentum. The right project makes learning feel natural, helps you understand how communities work, and gives you the confidence to keep going. In many ways, choosing the right project is more important than writing your first line of code.
When I began exploring open source, I assumed I just needed to pick a popular project and start contributing. In reality, it took some trial and error to understand what actually works for a beginner.
I explored a few projects in the Kubernetes ecosystem, including Kubebuilder and kubeadm. I learned from them, but I didn’t find a strong entry point to contribute meaningfully at that stage. Eventually I started looking into Cluster API, and things began to click. The issues were easier to follow, discussions were active, and I could see where I might fit in. That experience taught me something important: finding the right project is often about fit, not reputation.
Why Choosing the Right Project Matters
Your first contribution shapes your perception of open source.
If the experience feels approachable and structured, you are far more likely to stay consistent. It also helps you become familiar with workflows like issues, pull requests, and code reviews, which are just as important as the code itself.
This is why spending time exploring projects before contributing is not wasted effort. It is part of the process.
Why Popular Projects Can Feel Intimidating
Many beginners naturally gravitate toward well-known projects. That’s completely understandable, but large projects often have complex codebases, fast-moving discussions, and deeply technical issues. Without context, it can be hard to know where to begin.
This does not mean you should avoid them. It simply means you may need to observe first and contribute gradually.
Understanding Project Maturity (Especially in Cloud Native)
If you’re exploring the cloud-native space, you’ll notice that CNCF projects are categorized as Graduated, Incubating, and Sandbox. These maturity levels don’t mean one project is better than another. They mostly reflect adoption, stability, and ecosystem growth.
All of these projects are valuable. They simply differ in learning curves, community size, and the kinds of contributions you might start with.
________________


Most open source communities follow shared community guidelines and a code of conduct designed to keep the space welcoming and inclusive. Beginners are not outsiders. Everyone is encouraged to learn, ask questions, and contribute.
Look for communities where conversations are active, maintainers respond, and newcomers are supported.
Common Mistakes Beginners Make
Looking back, there are a few patterns I’ve noticed, both in my own journey and in conversations with others:
* Choosing a project only because it is famous
* Trying to solve a complex issue too early
* Ignoring documentation, discussions, and past pull requests
* Expecting to contribute immediately without observing the workflow
These are easy mistakes to make, and almost everyone goes through them at some point.
Observe Before You Contribute
One habit that helped me a lot was what I think of as shadowing.
Before picking up an issue, I would subscribe to issues and pull requests that looked interesting. I followed the discussions, watched how maintainers reviewed code, and tried to understand how decisions were made.
This gave me context without pressure. By the time I worked on something myself, the process already felt familiar.
Observation is underrated, but it can shorten the learning curve significantly.
A Simple Checklist for Choosing a Project
When evaluating a project, I usually look for a few signals:
* Are issues active and recently updated?
* Do maintainers respond to contributors?
* Are there labels like “good first issue” or “help wanted”?
* Is the documentation reasonably clear?
* Can I build or run the project locally?
If most of these answers are yes, it’s usually a good sign.
Start With What You Already Use
Another useful approach is to contribute to tools you already use. Familiarity reduces the initial barrier, and you are more likely to notice small improvements or gaps naturally.
Many contributions begin this way.
Be Patient With the Process
Finding the right project can take time. It might take days, or even months, to find a place where you feel comfortable contributing. That’s normal.
In my case, it took exploring multiple projects before I found one where I could contribute consistently. That period of exploration was not wasted time. It helped me understand how open source works and what kind of projects suited me best.
Your first project does not have to be perfect. It just needs to be a place where you can learn, participate, and gradually build confidence.

Open Source, One Step at a Time: Assigning the First Issue
Choosing wisely, committing thoughtfully, and building trust from day one
Choosing a project was the first step.
Shadowing contributors came next.
Now comes the moment where things shift.
You’re about to assign your first issue.
On the surface, it’s just a small action on GitHub. But in reality, it’s a quiet commitment. When you assign an issue to yourself, you’re signaling ownership. You’re saying, “I’m taking this forward.”
Let’s talk about how to do that well.
  
This is an AI generated image
1. Don’t Just Pick an Issue. Understand It.
Most people start by looking for labels like good first issue or help wanted. And that’s perfectly fine. Those labels exist to guide new contributors.
But labels are starting points, not guarantees.
Before assigning anything, slow down. Read the entire issue. Go through linked pull requests. Look at related discussions. Check when it was last updated. If it’s a bug, try to reproduce it. If it’s an enhancement, understand the context around it.
If you cannot clearly explain the issue in your own words yet, you’re not ready to assign it.
Clarity first. Assignment later.
2. Scope It Like a Finisher
Your first issue should be something you can complete with confidence.
If an issue mentions large refactors, redesigns, or touches multiple modules without clear direction, it may not be the right starting point. Long unresolved discussion threads are another signal that the scope might be unclear.
On the other hand, small, well-defined fixes are perfect. A reproducible bug. A documentation correction. A contained improvement. A version bump that follows a known pattern.
Ask yourself quietly:
“Can I see this through to completion?”
Your first contribution doesn’t need to be impressive. It needs to be finishable.
3. Break Down the Problem — Out Loud
Here’s something many new contributors don’t realize.
Not every issue is fully understood by maintainers.
Often, a user reports a bug. A maintainer triages it, adds labels, maybe confirms it’s valid, and leaves it open for contribution. But they may not have traced the root cause or designed the fix yet.
This is where your thinking matters.
Instead of immediately assigning the issue, comment your understanding. Explain what you think is happening. Share your rough approach. Ask if your interpretation aligns with expectations.
When you do this, you’re not just preparing to code. You’re helping clarify the issue itself.
Sometimes your comment becomes the missing structure the issue needed.
And even if you’re slightly wrong, visible reasoning moves the conversation forward.
4. Look for Prior Art
Before you start implementing anything, pause and look backward.
Has something similar been done before?
In open source, “prior art” refers to earlier work that solved a related problem. It might be a previous dependency bump, a similar bug fix, a related configuration change, or a pattern already established elsewhere in the codebase.
Maintainers sometimes link prior work directly in the issue description. If they do, read it carefully. That’s your blueprint.
If they don’t, search for it yourself. Look at closed pull requests. Scan commit history. Explore how similar logic was implemented in other modules.
You’ll often find that you’re not inventing a solution — you’re continuing a pattern.
Good contributors write code.
Great contributors read history.
5. Use the Community When You Need It
It’s normal to hesitate before commenting publicly. You might worry about misunderstanding something or asking a “basic” question.
But open source communities are built on conversation.
Most active projects have Slack workspaces, Discord servers, or discussion forums. Especially in ecosystems like CNCF projects, these spaces are active and welcoming to new contributors.
If you’re unsure, ask. Share what you’ve understood so far. Mention where you’re stuck. Explain what you’ve tried.
The difference between struggling alone and growing quickly is often just one question.
Asking doesn’t signal weakness. It signals intent.
6. Assign Only When You’re Ready
Assigning an issue is not about reserving it. It’s about committing to it.
1. Do it when the problem makes sense to you.
2. When the scope feels clear.
3. When your approach is reasonably shaped.
4. When you’re ready to begin.
That order matters.
7. After Assigning, Stay Present
This is the part that many contributors underestimate.
Once you assign an issue, you’ve taken ownership in a public space. Maintainers now assume it’s being handled.
But life happens. Work gets busy. Exams come up. Energy fluctuates.
The key isn’t speed. It’s communication.
You don’t need daily updates. But you do need visibility. A short weekly comment sharing progress, blockers, or timelines makes a huge difference.
Even something simple like:
“I’ve implemented part of the fix and will raise a PR next week.”
or
“I’m on a time crunch this week but will resume over the weekend.”
That kind of communication builds trust.
And if you truly can’t continue, say so. Offer to unassign. Transparency is respected far more than silence.
Open source runs on communication as much as code.
________________


A Flow to Remember
If you want a simple mental model, think of it this way:
Understand the issue.
 Clarify your thinking.
 Look for prior art.
 Commit with intention.
 Communicate consistently.
That’s how you assign your first issue responsibly.
________________


Assigning your first issue might feel small. But it’s the moment you move from observing to participating.
It’s where curiosity turns into contribution.
Where interest turns into responsibility.
And once that issue is yours, the next step is execution.

Open Source, One Step at a Time: Raising the First Pull Request
Why your first PR is less about perfection and more about clarity
I still remember the first time I hit “Create Pull Request.”
I must have reread everything at least five times. I scrolled through the diff again. I wondered if I had missed something obvious. I almost closed the tab once.
It’s a surprisingly vulnerable moment.
Your code is no longer just yours. It’s about to become visible to maintainers, reviewers, and future contributors. That can feel intimidating.
But over time, I’ve learned something important.
A pull request is not a submission. It’s a conversation.

A Pull Request Is a Conversation
When you open a PR, you’re not saying, “This is perfect.”
 You’re saying, “Here’s my approach. Let’s refine it together.”
That mindset shift changes everything.
Your goal isn’t to impress anyone. It’s to be clear. To explain what you did and why. To make it easy for someone else to understand your thinking.
That’s collaboration.
________________


Before You Raise the PR
One thing I always do before opening a PR is go back to the original issue.
Did I actually solve what was asked?
Or did I drift into improving other parts of the system?
It’s very easy, especially when you’re new and excited, to start redesigning things while fixing a small bug. I’ve done it. It rarely helps.
Solve the problem. Don’t redesign the system.
Small and focused is almost always better than ambitious and scattered.
Then I recheck the contribution guidelines. Every serious project has a CONTRIBUTING.md, and it’s worth reading carefully.
Look for things like:
* Branch naming conventions
* PR title format
* Commit message standards
* Required templates
* DCO sign-offs
* CLA requirements
Many beginners are unsure about DCO and CLA, so let’s simplify it.
A CLA (Contributor License Agreement) is usually a one-time agreement. It allows the project to legally use your contribution. You often sign it once through a link, and you’re done.
A DCO (Developer Certificate of Origin) is slightly different. It requires you to “sign off” your commits to confirm that you have the right to contribute the code.
In most cases, you can do this directly from the command line by adding the -s flag when committing:
git commit -s -m "Fix validation bug in config parser"
The -s automatically adds a line like:
Signed-off-by: Your Name <your@email.com>
Some projects enforce this strictly, and your PR won’t pass checks without it. It’s not personal. It’s just part of maintaining legal clarity in open source.
Spending five extra minutes here can save you a round of back-and-forth later.
________________


Review Your Own PR First
Before asking anyone else to review your work, sit with it yourself.
I usually follow what I call a quiet “rule of three.”
If I’m fixing a bug, I try to validate it in at least three different scenarios. Different inputs. Slightly different environments. Edge cases.
If it behaves correctly in all three, I feel more confident that I haven’t just patched one narrow case.
Then I read the diff slowly, almost as if it’s someone else’s code.
~ Would I question this variable name?
~ Did I update tests properly?
~ Did I run tests locally?
~ Did I accidentally include unrelated changes?
Sometimes I close the laptop and come back later. Fresh eyes catch small mistakes that tired eyes ignore.
Local tests should pass before CI ever sees your code. It shows respect for the reviewer’s time.
________________


Write a PR Description That Helps Future You
One of the biggest mindset shifts for me was realizing that a PR description is not just for the reviewer today.
It’s for the contributor who will come back to this file two years later and wonder, “Why was this change introduced?”
People often go back to old PRs to understand why something works the way it does. If your description clearly explains the problem and the reasoning behind your approach, you’re leaving behind context. That’s incredibly valuable.
So instead of writing “Fixed bug,” take a few minutes to explain:
What was happening?
Why was it happening?
What did you change?
How did you test it?
Use the project’s PR template if it exists. And then add thoughtful context where needed.
You’re not just merging code. You’re documenting a decision.
________________


Draft PRs Are More Powerful Than You Think
There was a time I felt like I had to “finish everything” before opening a PR. Now I often do the opposite.
Draft PRs are helpful when you want early feedback. Maybe you’re unsure about the direction. Maybe you want peers or friends in the same tech stack to glance at it. Maybe you just want CI checks to run while you continue working.
A draft PR doesn’t mean your work is incomplete. It means you’re open to feedback.
And that openness often leads to better outcomes with less rework.
________________


CI Failures Are Normal
When you open a PR, automated checks usually start running. Tests execute. Linters validate formatting. Builds run.
The first time a check fails, it can feel discouraging.
Don’t panic.
Read the logs carefully. They usually tell you exactly what went wrong. Fix it locally. Push again. Watch the checks rerun.
This is part of the workflow. Not a judgment of your skill.
________________


A Few Things to Watch Out For
If there’s one pattern I see often in first PRs, it’s overengineering.
Trying to make the solution more “complete” than necessary. Adding improvements that weren’t part of the issue. Expanding the scope just because you can.
Resist that.
Clear. Focused. Minimal. Well-explained.
That’s what makes a good first PR.
________________


Your First PR Won’t Be Perfect
And it doesn’t have to be.
No PR is perfect. Not the first one. Not the hundredth one.
What matters is that your change is thoughtful. That it solves the stated problem. That it invites conversation instead of shutting it down.
When you hit “Create Pull Request,” you’re not proving yourself.
You’re participating!
And once your PR is open, the next real learning begins: understanding and navigating review comments.

Open Source, One Step at a Time: Responding to Review Comments
What to Do When Review Comments Start Coming In
You raised your first PR.
 You waited.
 And then the review comments arrive.
  
This is an AI Generated picture
For many first-time contributors, this is the most nerve-wracking moment in the entire process. You start wondering if you missed something obvious. Maybe the code was not good enough. Maybe you misunderstood the issue.
Take a breath.
Review comments are not a judgment of you. They are simply the next step of collaboration.
In open source, a PR is rarely perfect on the first attempt. Reviews exist to make the code better, align it with the repository’s style, and help contributors learn how the project works.
If anything, review comments mean that someone took the time to carefully look at your work. That is a good sign.
________________


The First Reaction: Feeling Nervous is Normal
When the first review appears, many contributors immediately feel anxious. This is completely normal.
You might see suggestions about restructuring code, adding tests, or following a pattern already used in the repository. Sometimes the comments might reference parts of the codebase that you have never seen before.
That does not mean you did something wrong.
Maintainers know that contributors do not know the entire codebase. In fact, it is not expected. Reviews often bring in a perspective that only long-time contributors have.
Instead of reacting immediately, give yourself some time.
Read through the comments carefully.
Understand what the maintainer is suggesting.
If something is unclear, ask a question.
Most of the time, review comments are simply questions or suggestions. There is no hidden meaning behind them.
________________


Understanding Different Types of Review Comments
Not every comment means your solution is incorrect. Reviews often fall into a few common categories.
Sometimes maintainers suggest stylistic changes. Many repositories follow strict formatting rules or architectural patterns. Your code might work perfectly, but they want it to match the rest of the project.
Sometimes they ask for tests. If you add a new function or fix a bug, maintainers usually expect a unit test that proves the behavior.
Sometimes they ask for clarification. They might want to understand why you chose a particular approach.
And occasionally, they might suggest a better way to solve the problem.
All of this is part of the collaborative process. The goal is not to criticize the contributor. The goal is to improve the code that will live in the repository.
________________


Updating Your PR the Right Way
Once you understand the feedback, you can start updating your PR.
The easiest approach is to add small commits that address the review comments one by one. This keeps the history clear and helps reviewers see what changed.
Avoid force pushing while reviews are ongoing unless the maintainers specifically ask for it. Adding incremental commits makes it easier for everyone to follow the discussion.
It is also normal to have multiple rounds of review. Especially for your first PR.
One thing that helped me personally was learning to run the linters before pushing my changes. In my early contributions, I would often forget this step and the CI checks would fail. It happened enough times that I eventually built the habit.
Mistakes like these are common when you are starting out. You learn them quickly with practice.
When you address a comment and push the fix, you can reply to the comment explaining what you changed. If the issue is resolved, you can mark the conversation as resolved.
This helps keep the review thread clean and easier to follow.
________________


Reviews Can Take Time
Open source maintainers are often volunteers. Even when they are not, they might be reviewing contributions across different time zones and busy schedules.
Because of this, reviews may take time.
If you have addressed the comments and have not received a response for about a week, it is perfectly fine to politely follow up. Many projects have Slack, Discord, or discussion forums where contributors communicate.
A simple message like this is usually enough:
“Hi, I have addressed the review comments on this PR. Would love another look when you get time.”
Maintainers generally appreciate the reminder.
________________


Do Not Take Feedback Personally
Review comments can sometimes sound direct or blunt. That is simply the nature of written technical discussions.
Text does not always carry tone well.
The feedback is about the code, not about you.
The entire goal of the review process is to make sure the best possible solution ends up in the codebase. Everyone involved is working toward the same outcome.
Remember that maintainers want contributors to succeed. Healthy open source communities thrive on collaboration and learning.
________________


A Small Story From My Early Contributions
One of my early open source PRs received quite a few review comments.
At the time, I felt embarrassed. I thought maybe I had done everything wrong. Instead of responding immediately, I stepped away and did not touch that PR for almost a month.
Looking back, that was not the best decision.
During that time, I spoke to a few friends and colleagues who had more experience in the same tech stack. They looked at the comments and helped me understand them one by one. Most of the suggestions were actually small fixes or clarifications.
Once I finally returned to the PR, the changes were straightforward. But because I had disappeared for so long, the entire process took much longer than it needed to.
I later realized something important. Many new contributors abandon their PRs at the review stage simply because the comments feel intimidating.
If you take away one lesson from this post, let it be this.
Do not abandon your PR.
Every review is an opportunity to learn how the project works. Respond to comments thoughtfully, ask questions when things are unclear, and treat the process as collaboration rather than evaluation.

Open Source, One Step at a Time: After Your First PR Gets Merged
Your first PR got merged.
Take a moment. It matters.
It is not just a code change making its way into a repository. It is proof that you understood a problem, worked through an unfamiliar codebase, responded to feedback, and saw something through to completion.
Your first PR is not just a merge. It is a milestone.

This is an AI generated image
And before you do anything else, pause and acknowledge it.
Celebrate it in your own way. Share it with a friend, post about it, or just sit with that feeling for a bit. Open source is a long journey, and small wins like these are what keep you going.
Once that moment passes, you might feel an urge to jump into the next issue immediately. That is natural. But before you do that, take a small step back.
Go back to your PR.
Read through it once again. Look at the review comments, the changes you made, and how your initial approach evolved over time. There is a lot of learning hidden in that one contribution.
What did you miss the first time?
What did maintainers suggest?
What patterns did you start noticing?
Chances are, this is the only time you will revisit your first PR this closely. And it is worth doing.
You will notice small things. Maybe you could have structured the code better. Maybe you missed a test case. Maybe your PR description could have been clearer. None of this is a mistake. It is part of the process.
This reflection is what makes your next contribution better.
And yes, you are still a beginner.
One merged PR does not change that. But it gives you something more important than experience. It gives you confidence. You now know how to start, how to navigate the repository, how to respond to reviews, and how the entire flow works.
You are no longer starting from zero.
When you pick your next issue, try something slightly different.
If your first PR was about adding tests, maybe try fixing a small bug this time. If it was a bug fix, maybe explore documentation. If it was documentation, maybe try a small code change.
This is the best time to explore.
You are still new, which means you have the freedom to try different things without pressure. Over time, you will naturally find what you enjoy and where you want to go deeper.
Your next PR will likely feel a little easier. You will spend less time figuring out the basics and more time focusing on the actual problem. That is how momentum builds.
But do not turn that momentum into pressure.
You do not have to contribute every day. You do not have to rush. What matters is that you keep coming back.
The biggest shift after your first PR is not technical. It is mental.
The hesitation reduces. The fear of “what if this is wrong” becomes smaller. You start trusting yourself a little more.
And that is what really changes things.
Your first PR proves that you can contribute.
What you do next is where you grow.
